How to Test Your Mechanical Keyboard

Four steps to identify dead switches, chattering, and rollover issues on any mechanical or gaming keyboard.

01
Plug in & click the page
Connect your mechanical keyboard via USB or pair via Bluetooth. Click anywhere on this page to give the browser keyboard focus so it can detect your switch presses.
02
Press every switch once
Go row by row — Escape through F12, then the number row, letter rows, and finish with the numpad. Each switch turns gold on press and green once registered. Any switch staying dark is unresponsive.
03
Test rollover & ghosting
Hold 6+ keys simultaneously and check how many light up. Full NKRO keyboards register every key. If keys drop out when held together, you have limited rollover — important for fast gaming inputs.
04
Watch for chattering
Press each switch with a deliberate single tap and watch the Last Switch panel. A chattering switch fires multiple events from one press — visible as rapid repeated flashing. Cleaning or replacing the switch resolves this.

Common Mechanical Switch Issues

What this tester helps you diagnose and what to do about it.

💀
Dead Switch
Key never registers. Caused by a broken spring, bent pin, or failed solder joint. Hot-swap boards: reseat or swap the switch. Soldered boards: requires desoldering.
⚡
Chattering
Single press registers multiple times. Worn contacts inside the switch. Clean with contact cleaner spray or replace the switch. Common in older Cherry MX blues.
👻
Ghosting
Phantom keypresses appear when holding others. Common on budget membrane boards. Gaming keyboards with anti-ghosting matrix or NKRO circuitry eliminate this entirely.
🔒
Stuck Key
Key registers as permanently held. Debris under the keycap or a damaged spring. Remove the keycap, clean around the switch stem, and press the switch stem directly to test.
🎮
Limited Rollover
Keys drop out when multiple are held. 2KRO or 6KRO means missed inputs in fast-paced games. Look for keyboards advertising NKRO (N-Key Rollover) or 6KRO minimum for gaming.
📶
Intermittent Key
Key works sometimes but not always. Suggests a loose switch pin or cold solder joint. On hot-swap boards, remove and firmly reseat. On soldered boards, inspect solder points.

Mechanical Keyboard Test — FAQ

Common questions about diagnosing mechanical keyboards and gaming keyboards.

A dead switch produces no response at all on this tester — the key stays dark and the Last Switch panel doesn't update. A stuck switch often shows as a key that appears permanently pressed (the key stays gold/pressed state). You can distinguish them by pressing the switch stem directly without the keycap. If a hot-swap switch produces no response when pressed directly, the switch itself is dead and needs replacing.

Yes — fully compatible. Hot-swap keyboards (like those using Kailh hot-swap sockets or Gateron hot-swap PCBs) are tested identically to soldered keyboards. If a switch fails this test on a hot-swap board, simply pull the switch with a switch puller and replace it with a new one of your preferred type. No soldering required.

Chattering is when a mechanical switch's metal contacts bounce on closure, registering multiple keystrokes from a single physical press. Watch the Last Switch panel and the on-screen key — a chattering switch causes rapid repeated flashing. It is most common in clicky switches (like Cherry MX Blue) after heavy use. Isopropyl alcohol contact cleaner spray can temporarily fix chattering; replacing the switch is the permanent solution.

Hold down as many keys as you can simultaneously and count how many light up on the on-screen keyboard. True NKRO keyboards register every single key held regardless of count. 6KRO keyboards register up to 6 simultaneous keys — more than enough for most games. 2KRO keyboards (common on budget boards) only register 2 at once and will cause missed inputs in demanding scenarios. Note: some keyboards switch between 6KRO (USB) and NKRO (via a key combination) — check your manual.

Cherry MX Blue switches are the most prone to chattering due to their two-part stem design. First confirm it is chattering and not a software debounce issue by testing here — if the key registers multiple presses from one tap, it is hardware. Apply a small amount of isopropyl alcohol (90%+) contact cleaner under the switch housing through the side gap, actuate it several times, and let it dry. If it returns, the switch contacts are worn and the switch must be replaced or re-lubed with Krytox 205g0 on the stem rails.
