How to Use

A quick guide to getting the most accurate results.

01
Run this check before your typing test
Open this page and complete the key check before starting any typing speed test. A broken or sticky key will silently hurt your WPM score and accuracy without you realising it.
02
Press every key at typing speed
Don't press keys slowly — press them at the pace you would actually type. Some sticky or worn keys only miss inputs under normal typing pressure and rhythm.
03
Pay close attention to home-row keys
The keys A S D F G H J K L and the Space bar are the most frequently used in typing tests. Make sure each of these registers reliably and quickly before you begin.
04
Check the progress bar
The progress bar shows what percentage of your keys have been tested. Aim for 100% before any important typing assessment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions about keyboard health test for typists.

A faulty key — even one that misses inputs only occasionally — will silently reduce your WPM and inflate your error rate. You might assume you're a worse typist than you are, or fail a typing assessment unfairly. Running a 2-minute keyboard health check before any typing test ensures the hardware is not the limiting factor in your performance.

The home-row keys (A, S, D, F, G, H, J, K, L, semicolon) and Space bar are the most frequently struck in English typing tests and should be your first priority. After those, Shift (both sides), E, T, R, O, I, N — the highest-frequency letters in English — are critical. Backspace also matters if the test penalises uncorrected errors. This tester highlights all of these on the visual layout.

Significantly, yes. The Space bar is the single most frequently pressed key in any word-per-minute test. Even a 5% miss rate on the Space bar will cause missed word separations, inflating your error count and collapsing your WPM. If Space registers inconsistently on this tester, clean underneath it with compressed air or replace the stabiliser foam if your keyboard uses one.

A stuck key is one that physically doesn't spring back fully after being pressed, or one whose switch chatters (fires multiple keydown events per press). In a typing test, a chattering Space or letter key produces duplicate characters (e.g. typing 'the' gives 'tthe') which counts as errors and slows your WPM. On this tester, chattering appears as a key rapidly re-highlighting without being pressed again.

Yes — pressing every key systematically is a gentle warm-up routine that familiarises your fingers with the keyboard layout and gives each key a chance to loosen up. Many competitive typists do a quick key-press run as part of their pre-test ritual, and this tester gives you a visual confirmation that your fingers and the keyboard are both ready.
